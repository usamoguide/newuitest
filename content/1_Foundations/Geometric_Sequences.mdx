## Overview

Geometric sequences multiply by a constant ratio. They are the backbone of
exponential growth and infinite series.

## Key Ideas

- $g_n = g_1 r^{n-1}$.
- $S_n = g_1 \frac{1-r^n}{1-r}$ for $r \ne 1$.
- Infinite sums converge only when $|r| < 1$.

## Core Skills

### Find the Ratio

Compute $r=g_{n+1}/g_n$ from consecutive terms. Confirm it is constant.

### Use Finite vs. Infinite Sums

Apply $S_n$ only for finite sums. Use $S_\infty = g_1/(1-r)$ only when
$|r|<1$.

### Rewrite with Powers

Convert repeated multiplication into exponent form before simplifying.

## Worked Example

Find $1 + \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{9} + \cdots$.

This is geometric with $g_1 = 1$ and $r = \frac{1}{3}$. Since $|r| < 1$,
$S_\infty = \frac{1}{1-\frac{1}{3}} = \frac{3}{2}$.

## More Examples

### Example 1: Term Formula

If $g_1=6$ and $r=2$, find $g_5$.

$g_5 = 6\cdot 2^4 = 96$.

### Example 2: Finite Sum

Find the sum of the first 6 terms of $3,6,12,\ldots$.

$S_6 = 3\cdot (1-2^6)/(1-2) = 3(63) = 189$.

### Example 3: Convergence Check

Does $5 - 2.5 + 1.25 - \cdots$ converge? If so, find the sum.

$r=-1/2$ so $|r|<1$ and $S_\infty = 5/(1+1/2)=10/3$.

## Strategy Checklist

- Compute the ratio from consecutive terms.
- Decide whether the sum is finite or infinite.
- Check $|r|<1$ before using the infinite formula.
- Keep powers as $r^{n-1}$ to avoid off-by-one errors.

## Common Pitfalls

- Applying the infinite sum formula when $|r| \ge 1$.
- Mixing up $r^n$ vs. $r^{n-1}$ in the term formula.
- Using $r$ from non-consecutive terms without checking consistency.
