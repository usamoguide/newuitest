## Overview
 
The Pigeonhole Principle states that if you have more objects than containers, at least one container must hold
more than one object.
 
---

## Key Ideas
 
- First identify the "pigeons" (objects) and the holes (containers/categories).
- The number of holes is your bound. If pigeons $>$ holes, a contradiction is formed.
- For "at least $m$" ways, use the extended formula of, $\lceil k/n \rceil \geq m$, which means $k \geq n(m-1) + 1$.
- For specific range problems, one should partition the range into intervals and apply the
  pigeonhole princple to the subintervals that are created. 
- Note that the pigeonhole principle proves the existence only and hence does not tell you which hole
  is crowded or how to find the contradiction.
 
 
## Core Skills
 
### The Pigeonhole Principle
 
The Pigeonhole Principle states that if $n + 1$ or more objects are distributed into
$n$ containers, then at least one container contains $\mathbf{2}$ or more
objects.
 
Equivalently, if every container held at most $1$ object, the total number
of objects would be at most $n$. Since we have more than $n$, this is
impossible and hence the proof forms a contradiction. 
 
The most used example for this comes from the name itself. You have $k$ pigeons and $n$ holes. If $k > n$, at
least one hole has $\geq 2$ pigeons.
 
### Generalization - Also known as the extended formula of the principle 
 
If $k$ objects are distributed into $n$ containers, then at least one container contains at least $\left\lceil k/n \right\rceil$ objects.
 
Here $\lceil x \rceil$ is the ceiling function (also called the Gauss function in some countries), the smallest integer
$\geq x$.
 
An example of this would be $25$ students sit in $7$ rows. At least one row has
> $\lceil 25/7 \rceil = \lceil 3.57 \rceil = 4$ students.
 
### The Average across spaces
 
Another idea from this comes if the average value across $n$ containers is
$> m$, then at least one container has value $> m$. Furthermore, if the average is
$\geq m$, at least one container has value $\geq m$.
 
This is in cases, cleaner than actually invoking the ceiling formula directly.

An example of this would be when five numbers sum to $51$. Since the average is $51/5 = 10.2$,
hence at least one number is $\geq 11$.

## Worked Example
 
Show that among any $5$ integers, two must have the same
remainder when divided by $4$.
 
There are only $4$ possible remainders mod $4$: $\{0, 1, 2, 3\}$. These are the "holes", and the $5$ integers are the pigeons. Since $5 > 4$, by the Pigeonhole Principle, at least two integers share
a remainder. Their difference and is therefore divisible by $4$. $\blacksquare$

## More Examples
 
### Example 1 — A Basic application 
 
A drawer contains red, blue, and green socks. What is the minimum number of socks you must draw (without looking) to guarantee a matching pair?
 
The colors are the holes: $3$ holes. Each sock drawn is a pigeon. We know that by the Pigeonhole Principle, once you draw $3 + 1 = 4$ socks, two must share a color.
 
Answer: $\boxed{4}$.
 
 
---
 
### Example 2 — The Generalized Pigeonhole (Or extended)
 
There are $30$ students in a class. Show that at least $\lceil 30/12 \rceil = 3$ of them share a birth month.
 
There are $12$ months (holes) and $30$ students (pigeons). $$\left\lceil \frac{30}{12} \right\rceil = \left\lceil 2.5 \right\rceil = 3.$$
 
So at least one month contains $3$ or more students. $\blacksquare$ 

### Example 3 — Pigeonhole Principle in an interval 
 
Five points are chosen inside (or on the boundary of) a unit square. Show that two of them are within distance $\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$ of each other.
 
We divide the unit square into $4$ smaller squares of side $1/2$ by cutting it in half horizontally and vertically. These $4$ smaller squares are the would act as the "holes", 
and the five points would be the "piegons".
 
By pigeonhole, two points lie in the same small square. The maximum distance within a square of side $1/2$ is its diagonal, and thus
$\sqrt{(1/2)^2 + (1/2)^2} = \sqrt{1/2} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$.
 
So those two points are within $\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$ of each other.
$\blacksquare$

## Strategy Checklist

- Always write what are the "pigeons", What are the "holes", to avoid confusion. 
- Check if the number of pigeons strictly greater than the holes.
- For "sum/difference equals $c$" problems, pair up elements that sum or differ by $c$ and use those pairs as "holes" in our piegonhole principle. 


## Common Pitfalls

- The basic principle requires $n + 1$ "pigeons" for $n$ holes, not $n$. With exactly $n$ pigeons it is possible (though not
  guaranteed) that all holes are distinct. The guarantee of the contradiction will start at $n + 1$.
- Every "pigeon" must belong to exactly one hole. 
- The pigeonhole proves existence, not the specific contradiction formed within.
