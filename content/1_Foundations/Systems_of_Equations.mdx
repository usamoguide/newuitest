## Overview

Systems problems often hide symmetry. Look for sums and products that let you
replace two variables with easier ones.

## Key Ideas

- Substitution works well when one equation isolates a variable.
- Elimination cancels a variable by scaling and subtracting.
- Symmetric systems often reduce with $s=x+y$ and $p=xy$.

## Core Skills

### Choose a Method

If one variable is easy to isolate, substitute. If coefficients align, use
elimination. If the system is symmetric, use $s$ and $p$.

### Track Extraneous Solutions

Squaring or multiplying equations can introduce extra solutions; check every
candidate in the original system.

### Use Vieta When Quadratic Appears

If $x$ and $y$ satisfy $t^2-st+p=0$, then the solutions are the roots $t$.

## Worked Example

Solve $x+y=5$ and $xy=6$.

Let $t$ be a root of $t^2 - 5t + 6 = 0$ by Vieta. Then
$(t-2)(t-3)=0$, so $t=2$ or $t=3$. Thus $(x,y)=(2,3)$ or $(3,2)$.

## More Examples

### Example 1: Elimination

Solve $2x+3y=13$ and $4x-3y=5$.

Add: $6x=18$, so $x=3$, then $y=7/3$.

### Example 2: Symmetric System

Solve $x+y=4$ and $x^2+y^2=10$.

$x^2+y^2=(x+y)^2-2xy$ gives $10=16-2xy$, so $xy=3$.
Then $t^2-4t+3=0$, so $t=1$ or $3$.

### Example 3: Check Extraneous

Solve $x+y=3$ and $xy=4$.

$t^2-3t+4=0$ has no real roots, so no real solutions.

## Strategy Checklist

- Pick substitution, elimination, or symmetry first.
- Convert symmetric systems to $s$ and $p$.
- Check all solutions in the original equations.

## Common Pitfalls

- Dropping solutions when squaring or substituting.
- Forgetting to check against the original equations.
- Mixing up which equation to substitute.
