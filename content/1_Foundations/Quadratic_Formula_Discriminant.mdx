## Overview

The quadratic formula solves any quadratic and the discriminant tells you how
many real solutions it has.

## Key Ideas

- $x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}$.
- The discriminant $\Delta = b^2-4ac$.
- $\Delta > 0$ gives two real roots, $\Delta=0$ gives one, and $\Delta<0$ gives none.
- Sum and product of roots: $r+s=-b/a$, $rs=c/a$.

## Core Skills

### Normalize the Quadratic

Write the equation as $ax^2+bx+c=0$ before applying the formula.

### Use the Discriminant First

Check $\Delta$ to know how many real solutions to expect and whether factoring
is possible.

### Reduce Fractions Early

Simplify $\sqrt{\Delta}$ if it is a perfect square to avoid messy arithmetic.

## Worked Example

Solve $2x^2 - 3x - 2 = 0$.

Compute $\Delta = (-3)^2 - 4(2)(-2) = 9 + 16 = 25$.
So $x = \frac{3 \pm 5}{4}$, giving $x=2$ or $x=-\frac{1}{2}$.

## More Examples

### Example 1: One Real Root

Solve $x^2-6x+9=0$.

$\Delta = 36-36=0$, so the only root is $x=3$.

### Example 2: No Real Roots

How many real roots does $x^2+2x+5=0$ have?

$\Delta = 4-20=-16<0$, so there are no real roots.

### Example 3: Factoring Check

Solve $x^2-5x+6=0$.

$\Delta=25-24=1$, so roots are $(5\pm 1)/2$ giving $x=2,3$.

## Strategy Checklist

- Move all terms to one side first.
- Compute $\Delta$ to predict the number of real roots.
- Simplify $\sqrt{\Delta}$ before dividing by $2a$.
- Check solutions in the original equation if needed.

## Common Pitfalls

- Dropping the negative sign on $b$.
- Forgetting to divide by $2a$.
- Treating $\sqrt{b^2-4ac}$ as $b-2ac$.
