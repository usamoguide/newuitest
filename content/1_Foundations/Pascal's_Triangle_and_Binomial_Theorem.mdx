## Overview
 
Pascal's triangle and the binomial theorem both come from the same idea. Every entry in the triangle is a binomial coefficient $\binom{n}{k}$, and the binomial theorem tells how those coefficients appear when expanding 
$(a+b)^n$. These are both fast ways to compute counting problems, identify patterns, and prove identities that would otherwise require long and bashy algebra.
 
## Key Ideas
 
- The $n$th row of Pascal's triangle lists $\binom{n}{0}, \binom{n}{1}, \ldots, \binom{n}{n}$, in symmetry.
- Pascal's identity: $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k-1} + \binom{n-1}{k}$.
- Symmetry from the Pascal Triangle forms $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{n-k}$.
- Sum of rows $\binom{n}{0} + \binom{n}{1} + \cdots + \binom{n}{n} = 2^n$.
- The hockey stick identity: $\binom{r}{r} + \binom{r+1}{r} + \cdots + \binom{n}{r} = \binom{n+1}{r+1}$.
- Binomial theorem: $(a+b)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k$.
- To find a specific coefficient in $(a+b)^n$, $\binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k$ shows the general term.

## Core Skills
 
### Pascal's Triangle
 
First, what is the Pascal's Triangle and why is it so important? Each entry is the sum of the two entries directly above it. Row 0 starts with
just 1. Writing out a few rows, we get 
 
$$
\begin{array}{ccccccccc}
& & & & 1 & & & & \\
& & & 1 & & 1 & & & \\
& & 1 & & 2 & & 1 & & \\
& 1 & & 3 & & 3 & & 1 & \\
1 & & 4 & & 6 & & 4 & & 1
\end{array}
$$
 
Row $n$ has $n+1$ entries, and the $k$th entry (starting from $k=0$) is
$\binom{n}{k}$. This comes from Pascal's identity, to choose $k$ items from $n$,
either the last item is included (choose $k-1$ from the rest) or it isn't
(choose $k$ from the rest).
 
### Identity of Symmetry 
 
$$\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{n-k}.$$
 
Choosing $k$ items to include is the same as choosing $n-k$ items to exclude.
On the triangle, row $n$ reads the same forwards and backwards, hence forming this equation. 
 
### Row Sum
 
$$\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} = 2^n.$$
 
Every subset of an $n$-element set either includes or excludes each element, and hence 
giving $2^n$ subsets total. Another way is to substitute $a=b=1$ into the binomial
theorem.
 
### Hockey Stick Identity
 
$$\sum_{i=r}^{n} \binom{i}{r} = \binom{n+1}{r+1}.$$
 
On the triangle, if one starts at any $\binom{r}{r} = 1$ on the diagonal and sum
diagonally downward, the result is the entry one step down and one step right.
People call it the "hockey stick identity" because the shape traced looks like a hockey stick.
 
### Binomial Theorem/Expansion 
 
$$(a+b)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k.$$
 
Each term $\binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k$ comes from choosing which $k$ of the $n$
factors contribute a $b$ (and the rest contribute an $a$). The coefficient of
$a^{n-k} b^k$ is exactly $\binom{n}{k}$. This can be shown on the Pascal Triangle directly. 
 
To find a specific term, identify the $k$ that gives the powers that we want, and then 
compute $\binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k$ directly. Hence we see that there is no need to expand everything.
 
### Substitution Tricks
 
Plugging specific values into $(a+b)^n = \sum \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k$ creates useful identities that we use in Combinatorics. 
 
- $a=b=1$: $\quad \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} = 2^n$.
- $a=1, b=-1$: $\quad \sum_{k=0}^{n} (-1)^k \binom{n}{k} = 0$.
- $a=1, b=2$: $\quad \sum_{k=0}^{n} 2^k \binom{n}{k} = 3^n$.
 
## Worked Example
 
Find the coefficient of $x^3$ in the expansion of $(2x + 3)^5$.
 
By the binomial theorem, the general term is $\binom{5}{k}(2x)^{5-k}(3)^k$.
We need $5 - k = 3$, so $k = 2$.
 
$$\binom{5}{2}(2x)^3(3)^2 = 10 \cdot 8x^3 \cdot 9 = 720x^3.$$
 
Thus, coefficient of $x^3$ is $\boxed{720}$.
 
When finding a single term, always isolate $k$ from the exponent condition
first, and then substitute. It would not be ideal to expand the full expression.
 
## More Examples
 
### Example 1: Pascal's Identity
 
Show that $\binom{8}{3} = \binom{7}{2} + \binom{7}{3}$.
 
$$\binom{7}{2} + \binom{7}{3} = 21 + 35 = 56 = \binom{8}{3}. \checkmark$$
 
This is Pascal's identity with $n=8$, $k=3$. We know that it works because every 3-element
subset of $\{1,\ldots,8\}$ either contains 8 (choose 2 from the remaining 7)
or does not (choose 3 from 7).
 
### Example 2: Symmetry
 
Evaluate $\binom{100}{97}$.
 
$$\binom{100}{97} = \binom{100}{3} = \frac{100 \cdot 99 \cdot 98}{3!} = 161700.$$
 
Always use symmetry to make the smaller number the bottom. This tip is very important for general problems in combinatorics. 
### Example 3: Row Sum
 
A pizza shop offers 10 toppings. How many different pizzas can you order
(including the plain pizza with no toppings)?
 
Each topping is either included or not: $2^{10} = 1024$ pizzas.
 
This is the row sum $\sum_{k=0}^{10} \binom{10}{k} = 2^{10}$. In other words, every pizza
corresponds to a subset.
 
### Example 4: Hockey Stick
 
Evaluate $\binom{2}{2} + \binom{3}{2} + \binom{4}{2} + \binom{5}{2} + \binom{6}{2}$.
 
By the hockey stick identity with $r=2$ and $n=6$:
 
$$\sum_{i=2}^{6} \binom{i}{2} = \binom{7}{3} = 35.$$
 
### Example 5: Substitutions 
 
Prove that the sum of binomial coefficients with alternating signs is zero:
$\binom{n}{0} - \binom{n}{1} + \binom{n}{2} - \cdots = 0$ for $n \geq 1$.
 
Substitute $a=1$, $b=-1$ into $(a+b)^n$:
 
$$(1-1)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} (-1)^k = 0. \checkmark$$

 
## Strategy Checklist
 
- Write out a few rows of Pascal's triangle when the problem involves small $n$, if not memorized. 
- Use symmetry $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{n-k}$ to put the smaller number on the bottom, and thus reduce heavy calculations. 
- For a single term in $(a+b)^n$, find $k$ from the exponent condition first.
- To sum all coefficients, substitute $a = b = 1$ (or $x = 1$).
- Try to recognize hockey stick sums consecutive $\binom{i}{r}$ along a diagonal, as some problems will deliberately hide them from you. 
- When a row sum looks like $2^n$ or $3^n$, try using substitution. 
 
## Common Pitfalls
 
- In Pascal Triangle, or for any combinatoric diagram or sequence, mislabeling rows can be a common mistake. For instance, Row 0 is the top row (just a 1), not row 1.
- Forgetting to raise the full term: in $(2x+3)^5$, the $a$ is $2x$, so $a^{n-k} = (2x)^{n-k}$, not $2x^{n-k}$.
- Cases of misapplying symmetry. $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{n-k}$, not $\binom{k}{n-k}$.
- Summing rows incorrectly can be another crucial mistake. Row $n$ has $n+1$ entries and sums to $2^n$, not $2^{n-1}$.
